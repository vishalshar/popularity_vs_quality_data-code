About the Author

Gail Simone is an American writer of comic books. Best known for penning DC's Birds of Prey, her other notable works include Secret Six, Welcome to Tranquility, The All-New Atom, and Deadpool. In 2007, she took over Wonder Woman. In 2011, she became the writer for Batgirl as well as co-writer for The Fury of Firestorm. Subsequently, she has left both titles--leaving Firestorm in Feb 2012 after 6 issues,[2] as well as being fired off Batgirl in December 2012 by the title's incoming editor, Brian Cunningham.[3] By December 21, Simone was back writing Batgirl The author lives in Florence, OR..

4.0 out of 5 stars"Leaving Megalopolis"...
ByA. Ireyon October 25, 2014
Format: Hardcover|Verified Purchase
"Leaving Megalopolis" was a crowd funded project teaming together Gail Simone, writer most well known for her stint on "Birds of Prey" and her run on "Batgirl", and artist Jim Calafiore, who has done the art for "Deadpool" and "Exiles". It's a rather interesting project since the book involves superheroes but is not about them. The story is based around the idea that all the superheroes of a particular city have gone rogue and are now killing people. The beginning of the book is set some time after most of the city has gone to hell and what follows is not like many other comic books out there. Rather, we are treated to a story that is about the ordinary people of the city and how they have come to deal with this situation.

As you would imagine, Simone does a great job in creating deep characters. While only one character's history is explored in depth it isn't difficult to imagine the kind of life that was led by certain characters prior to the current scenario based on what hints are dropped. What Simone did in this particular book was create both strong female and male characters, all of whom shine for a bit in the series. At least in some meaningful way if there is enough time given to them. Frankly, there are times when I thought that I would get an interesting character only for said character to be killed within a few short panels. The superheroes themselves aren't extremely unique but again, this is a story about the ordinary people in this situation.

As you might imagine, given the dark nature of the book the art is rather dark as well. Calafiore's art is fantastic and suits the tone of the book very well. It's dark and gritty but has enough of the shine and attractiveness that you would expect out of a superhero comic book. I should warn people up front that the book does not shine away from deaths as you may expect. Rather, it shows them in their full glory and, depending on your level of comfort, this could deter you. Some deaths are quite graphic. Frankly, I was pleasantly surprised but I suppose I had some expectations of this given the dark nature of the story.

I will say, the book feels awfully short. It isn't a short book in comparison to other compiled volumes but I think it has to do with the pace of the story. Before I really noticed the time I was done with the book in one sitting, which speaks to how interesting I found it. If there was one complaint I had about the book is that ending leaves off on a point where things truly get interesting. It almost felt like I was getting the rug pulled from underneath me and I was left with this sense of wanting more. Given how this story came from a crowd funded origin perhaps more funds will lead to more stories. I would also hope for more closure for why the incident occurs in the first place. The reader is given a point to go off of but the reasons behind why this event made the heroes go rogue isn't explained in great detail and forces the reader to make their own conclusions on the "science" of the turn.

Overall, it's a fun read. I would recommend it to people who are looking to read a more dark story about how ordinary people deal with superheroes who aren't quite heroes anymore. Again, I warn people against the abrupt ending but if you can get past that you should have fun with this title.

5.0 out of 5 starsGREAT title, nice release, includes Wonderboy
ByThatUmbrellaGuyTOP 1000 REVIEWERVINE VOICEon October 21, 2016
Format: Hardcover
This review will contain spoilers. Please SKIP this review AFTER THIS OPENING (I will note spoilers to reinforce where) if you have not read the series, knowing it is good enough to keep your interest and that this set covers in a lovely hardcover edition. This edition collects all six comics in the series, and it also adds the routine items you expect afterward.

Gail Simone and Jim Calafiore really outdid themselves when it came to this, and the Kickstarter that ultimately got this funding showcases how, despite the problems Kickstarter has all-too-often, that Kickstarter sometimes does great and wonderful things, too.

If you do not know Gail Simone, she is probably best known for her work with Batgirl and Birds of Prey. I personally like her storylines, too, because they oftentimes add a lot to the characters and to struggles people endure, although you REALLY have to watch her politics in some of her titles. Batgirl is a good example, with some of the work there highlighting everything good that an effective team can do if motivated, and also what happens when you give someone the idea of modernity and a soapbox and a few minutes with ideas like transgenderism. I accordingly comment on her works and on the content, and I am pretty happy to say that this is more survival-oriented with a twist of alternative coupling than anything truly trying to pitch a point (and miss).

Jim Calafiore is someone I knew for his work on Exiles and on Aquaman, and I have to say that I really liked his work here. The workld he details is harrowing and bleak, and I think he captures that both in landscape and in the characters he depicts. If you check out proofs of his speedster, for example, you can see what kind of details he added in to give you all of the 'right wrong,' if you will, with his look showcasing both a 'before' while also asking you about the 'now' and what is happening. I also love what the speedster does with the marker - to me, that is a terrifying thought, because you know he could pretty much do anything he wants. The bridge scene and the reason it is intimidating really shines as well; you can look these up, although I would encourage someone wanting a surprise to simply pick this up, enjoy the use shadows and motion like those in the beginning, and see how those first few pages sit. If you like them, it only gets better.

SPOILERS - STOP HERE.

After reading Surviving Megalopolis, I decided to return to Leaving Megalopolis and give it another read, plus I decided I needed to go out and pick up a spare copy to give as a primer to friends that I will be recommending Surviving Megalopolis to. I will NOT spoil the second item, but I will say that the two tales connect together well and that I could see a third coming soon. Basically, this first entry is more of a 'survive the horror' type of title, with our main character pretty much looking at a world-gone-wrong scenario just like we are, with awe and a bit of 'how in the world do I get out of this' tattooed across her face. From the beginning, we see what she is up against, with Megalopolis producing heroes in the past that people knew and loved, and something going terribly wrong. I like the way the ex-heroes are introduced, too, because their looks and powers really make the vulnerability of the human population all-the-more real. As we follow along, we pick up more people, look at some of what transpired (not much, but some), and we see a little of the world both inside the city and beyond. We also get a taste of how people would be, and some of the madness they produce; let's just say that it really does earn the term 'madness.'

The graphic novel also includes an extra story, Wonderboy, which is another tale from the broken city, Megalopolis. I personally enjoyed its inclusion, because it looks at what a hero is now, versus what heroes were in the past. It also deals with themes like sacrifice, and change, and all in the glossy pages of a world gone mental.

This set also includes a sketch pad in the back and some character / story development, which is always interesting to see in the back pages because it gives you a view of the process behind the story.

Again, I really like this series and think it is worth picking up. Surviving Megalopolis is also great, and it adds to what is already a great series. If you like horrific titles and items like Irredeemable, you really should check this out.
