Holographic. Multi-room. Portable

Where did you get the inspiration for your product?

The Core is the product of five years of intense research and development that started with one very simple question: ‘Why can’t a small beautiful speaker sound large and life-like?’ Working in collaboration with a community of innovative scientists, engineers, designers, musicians and creative partners, the Core was developed with three key tenets in mind: functional design, stunning sound and freedom for the listener. We leveraged cutting edge technologies such as Wave Field Synthesis, high-density microprocessors and high definition wireless protocols to bring you the most advanced wireless speaker system ever developed.

What differentiates your product from similar products out there?

Mass Fidelity is a pioneer in "Wave Field Synthesis" a technology used in the Core to create an astounding holographic image with universal sweet spot that sounds like two separate high fidelity speakers coming from one small box. That sonic realism combined with its portability, and no-hassle multi-room capability make the Core stand out from the crowd.

Tell us about the best and most challenging parts of the creation process.

The Core is the first product to combine several cutting edge technologies. Getting them to work together seamlessly while providing a simple user experience was a huge challenge, but achieving the goal was immensely gratifying. Simple is hard.

4.0 out of 5 starsSmall portable BT speaker with good features, some caveats for me
ByTim Kimzeyon December 23, 2015
Core, a cube-shaped box (6"x 6" wide, 4" high) that is a bluetooth speaker, is produced by Mass Fidelity, a hi-fi company based in Canada. The Core was funded for mass-production through Indiegogo, where i first heard of the product and became an early backer.

The Core houses 5 speaker drivers, two full-range units on the front, and one on each side, as well as a downward-firing sub for the bass impact.
The main selling point of the speaker is the touted holographic-like sound dispersion of the stereo effect. That was my main reason for purchase.
After the wait of over a year, the product is finally on the market and being delivered to backers.

SOUND:
It does produce a good amount of sound, certainly room-filling. Upon first listen, i thought the BASS response was horrendously bloated, and not in a good way. It was boomy, and at higher volumes would distort and vibrate the box (and the mid-highs did not seem nearly as loud). As i use PowerAmp on my Android phone, i have various EQ settings for each device i use (a myriad of IEMs, headphones, speakers, car stereo) and immediately tuned the EQ in the ranges i felt flattened out the sound for a more accurate response. But, I've actually found this hard to do with the Core, as the bass is STILL ever-present. Almost like the bass driver is a transducer--although i think the system is somehow tuned electronically as well (not just in a passive cross-over)--as MassFidelity is producing a companion subwoofer for the Core--which should change the bass tuning of the Core when connected--i believe the bass tuning is just not as adjustable as it should be, and is over-blown out of the box. There is a wired SUB-out (3.5mm jack) which will pair with any Sub. Upon testing, i hear that this output seems to be full-range, meaning there is no cutoff frequency. For me, that's a good thing, as i can connect the Core to other speakers for full-output as well. Most subs have their own X/O anyway.

TREBLE response is good, and reveals lots of detail. It is clean with minimal sibilance and harshness. But i still find the overall sound a bit thin (mostly as the bass is over-blown) and lacking in true mid-range spectrum. I will listen and "burn-in" the speaker before i make a final judgement, but just feel that the overall tuning is not to my own personal liking (I have training as a recording engineer and also build by own speaker systems). Even solo piano recordings (which have very high dynamic range) are fairly well presented. Acoustic guitar, bluegrass, female vox are bright and articulate in presentation.

Unfortunately, i just don't feel the main feature--the "Holographic Sound--is that well implemented. I understand--and have been conducting several tests--that placement can be crucial, but so far it hasn't produced the far-placed traditional L/R speaker effect that it is supposed to be able to simulate. It can have a bit of a spacial quality, particularly as close (near-field) listening, but not to the degree i was led to believe. I have been listening for hours now, with many genres of music, and tracks that have a very good stereo mixing, as well as exaggerated stereo effect (the early stereo versions of some Beatles' tracks for instance, Pink Floyd's "Money", etc) and some do offer much more effect than others, but nothing that makes me feel the sound is coming from a different area/plane of focus. So, as yet I am a bit disappointed in the STEREO IMAGING. It does have better stereo imaging than many other small bluetooth speakers, for its size, and the relative distance from the drivers, but the specific "holographic" effect is not overly discernible, and generally the sound still feels as if its from one source to me.

The BUILD quality seems to be very good. Its a small, slick plastic box with a chrome base, and it has good weight and heft for its size. The CONTROLS work very well. I'd wish the volume control had more indication as to the level of the setting. The LED will blink upon up or down button pushed, and when the up button is pushed to the max position, stop blinking. The top plate houses NFC for quick bluetooth pairing. The 4 slightly-indented push-buttons control Multi-room (for connecting/linking multiple Core speakers together, LED turns purple in this mode), Input: Bluetooth (blue LED), Wired (3.5mm jack) audio input (white LED), Optical audio input (orange LED). It is also capable of use as a speakerphone (the LED then turns green), but i found that you have to first enable this feature by holding down the power button and the input button simultaneously for a few seconds. You may then have to re-pair your phone. [Another feature which initially was to be on the Core was Gesture Control, whereby you could wave your hand across the device to change tracks. This feature was taken off just before production, but I still think it would have been a unique and useful feature if implemented].

BLUETOOTH: My phone (Note3) paired immediately with the Core with no problem whatsoever, and has held a connection well. Range seems decent enough, as i can walk around a large room and into other close rooms without losing connection. However, i am experiencing lots of micro-dropouts when playing music (milli-second losses of sound) that can be rather annoying. It's hard to say if this is entirely the fault of the Core, and i also experience it with other BT devices, but so far it does seem more frequent with the Core. If your phone is set for messaging and other interruptions, that will affect performance and is entirely the phone's fault however. You can change your phone's settings to block these while listening to music, which is preferable. RF noise is minimal so the receiver seems well shielded. Also, "black" sound has a good low-noise floor, even when no music is playing, with minimal amp buzz/hiss.

The Core also makes intentional little noises of its own. When powering up, when obtaining a BT connection, etc. They are not loud, piercing, or annoying as many other devices are, and can be almost cute--more importantly, unobtrusive to the function of the device. The main LED will tell you battery level (flashing red when its low, solid red when on DC power cord) and flashes blue when connecting to bluetooth. There is another LED in the back as well, beside the main power button, that tells you the charge status (yellow when charging, green when fully charged). The Core will also power itself off, but only when music has not been played for some time, to conserve battery life.

BATTERY life is very respectable at a claimed 12 hours. I haven't tested it fully (battery cells can change upon initial charge/recharge cycles), but it seems to get at least 9-12 hours at moderate volume playing. There is a USB plug in the back, which the battery can also power/charge a device with. I had no problem almost fully charging my phone (3200mAh battery) while at the same time playing music and using my phone--this is a very nice feature, and seems to put out enough amps to do so easily. Of course, this will drain your battery faster, but seems to work great, is convenient, and the battery still has good music-playing life.

It comes in a well-thought package with a simple remote control (power, volume, input), AC power connectors for various countries which then fit into the standard transformer box to power/charge the Core, a RCA to stereo 3.5mm jack, a soft carry bag, a cleaning wipe (yes, the top plate is particularly a finger-print magnet), and a small get-started printed instruction guide. These are not very detailed, but you can find the full manual on Mass Fidelity's website, here:
http://massfidelity.com/support/
You can then download the full user guide, which is a PDF file.

Overall, its a well-thought out and designed speaker, with a lot of room-filling sound in a small portable package. I'm just not sure the sound signature is to my personal liking, nor particularly that the stereo imaging feature works as well as was hoped. I'll continue to test and add/modify my review as needed. Hope this has been of some help!

Update 1/12/2016

(I had written an update about a week after the original review, but it seems that it was not cached/saved to the review, so i am writing my updated observations now, after a few weeks of using the Core)

The initial bloated bass diminished after several hours of use. I can safely say that the sound signature absolutely changed as i had EQ profiles saved for the Core to try to flatten out the bass response from the beginning. The bass was so bloated initially that it was distorting and vibrating the box quite noticeably. I had moved the lower registers of my EQ almost to nil using PowerAmp (Bands at 31, 62, and 125Hz) and the bass was still boomy. Now, after many hours, i have raised those bands up significantly to a flatter line, and am getting much less bloated bass response and little distortion. In other words, the bass has improved greatly and is more balanced with the rest of the sound spectrum. I cant say if the factor is just a "burn-in" effect or if the Core's processing has changed, but it has without a doubt changed after several hours of use. Frankly, if it had not i would not have been very happy at all. Hopefully those testing/writing reviews will take this into consideration though.

I am still having some issues with micro-dropouts of the sound with the BT signal. I'm not sure what is causing it, as the effective range seems standard, and range limits do not seem to be the determinant factor for the dropouts. Its hard to rule out my own devices as at least partly contributors, but i have also been testing two of my tablets (along with my smartphone) and still get some dropouts, so i do fear there is some problems with the bluetooth receiver to some extent.

There have been some discussions in regards to the optical input on the Core. I've read that some users are experiencing problems with the optical jack not fitting their cords, or the jack not properly seating in the Core. I have used three different brands of optical cables (one of them a very inexpensive AmazonBasics and another high-end braided cable) and have had no issues whatsoever with my optical port. It works perfectly and fits easily and securely, so i am not sure if there is a QC problem with some jacks or if certain cords are just a compatibility issue.

I have also been successful at hearing the "holographic" effect of the stereo imaging! After many attempts at positioning the Core within various room environments, i have been able to hear a pronounced imaging effect that envelops the listener. However, the effect isn't generated all the time, with every recording, and isn't easily repeatable without exacting environments. When it did get the effect to work, it is enjoyable and interesting, but after an hour or so of listening, i also find it a bit fatiguing. I believe the holographic effect is greatly dependent on room factors. In particular, itneeds reflective surfaces to bounce the sound back to the listening position. I've never been able to attain the "holographic" sound in a neutral or "soft" room environment, such as my living room. Most rooms have a combination of "hard" (more highly reflective) and "soft" (more highly absorbant) surfaces. The harder the areas, the more reflection--a room with a lot of soft materials (curtains, drapes, furniture) will absorb more HF sound; a studio environment would negate reflective soundwaves with acoustical foam making it neutral.
In that respect, i still find the "holographic" imaging to be a bit disappointing, and not what i had expected given the marketing information.

Nonetheless, i'm still impressed with the overall sound quality given the size of the ONE BOX and the minimal distance between the drivers creating the stereo imaging. For my personal use--as a PORTABLE bluetooth speaker, it does a fine job. It is a small, one piece device that is battery-operated, and that is the main way i will be using mine.

3.0 out of 5 starsAmazingly good sound from a single little box, but losing points with updates
ByNpsychon November 26, 2015
As one of the very first backers of their Indiegogo campaign, I received my Core yesterday. Out of the box, it paired rapidly via NFC and has remained paired. It came with several different adapters for different voltage electric systems, a remote control with buttons for volume and source for use a soundbar, and a soft grey drawstring bag. I have not fully tested the bluetooth range, but it seems solid. It charged in less than 2 hours and I have not yet tested the claimed battery life. I also haven't tested any inputs other than bluetooth. Now, on to the sound:

I haven't had the opportunity to test multiple high end bluetooth speakers. That being said, the sound coming out of this system is truly excellent. I'm not sure that I would say it fills the room (tested in a 30 x 15 ft living room), but it does create a wide and rich soundfield from a single little box. The highs are as crisp as can be. Listening to some of my favorite songs (Thievery Corporation, Emancipator) was like a new experience as additional sonic detail was revealed. The mids are excellent and drums sound like they're in the room with you. The bass response is quite good for such a little box. I do have the yet-to-be released subwoofer coming when production begins. However, bass is quite satisfying as is. The Core can get quite loud (and then truly does fill the room, but you might not want to be right next to it) and when you pump up the volume there isn't even the slightest hint of distortion in any of the ranges. I am very very impressed with this system. I can see how having a couple Cores linked would truly up the ante in terms of sound imaging, but I'm not sure I really need that.

Overall, the sound this system puts out is amazing. But, keep in mind that I haven't had a Sonos or similar system with which to compare.

UPDATE: 12/2/15.
I'm still very pleased with this little box. The bluetooth range (using a 2015 Moto x Pure) is great. No problems at 20+ft through multiple walls/doors. Two things I did notice: (1) a heated plastic smell coming from the box after playing it at high-ish volume for a while. I don't think this is terribly unusual and it is not very strong. (2) the battery life may not be quite what it's claimed. I haven't tracked it precisely, but my estimation is that I get about 10 hours from it before it beeps to tell me it's running low. This is a mix of low volume (studying level) and high volume (workout sessions), probably about a 40/40 mix with some medium volume time making up the rest.

I still think that the sound is excellent for what this is, and really, excellent period. However, I'm not sure I'd pay $600 for it (I got it for about half of that) without a lot of in-person research indicating superiority over somewhat cheaper competitors.

UPDATE 12/31/15: I've been using this as a sound bar with the TV for a few weeks. It's connected via am optical cable out from the TV. Sound is good, but the controls are a serious problem. The remote input for the Core interacts with my Xfinity remote in very inconsistent ways. Sometimes the volume up on the Xfinity remote increases the volume on the core, sometimes it decreases it, sometimes it switches between the Core audio input sources. This is extremely frustrating. Maybe it is my TV (a newer Samsung plasma) but I've never experienced anything like this. To me, this is really unacceptable for such an expensive piece of audio equipment.

Also, the Bluetooth reliability and range doesn't seem as good as previously observed.

I'm beginning to wish I didn't wait so long and purchased something else.
